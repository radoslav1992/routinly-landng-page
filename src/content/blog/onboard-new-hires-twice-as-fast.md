Onboarding at most companies is an oral tradition. A new hire joins, and for two weeks a senior teammate performs the same live walkthroughs they performed for the last hire: the CRM, the deploy process, the expense tool, the weird spreadsheet that runs payroll.

It works — but it scales terribly. Every new hire costs a senior person days of repeated explanation, the quality depends on who's giving the tour, and nothing is written down for the questions that come three weeks later.

## Record the walkthrough you were already giving

Here's the switch that changes the economics: the next time you give a walkthrough, record it. Not a polished tutorial — the actual walkthrough, exactly as you'd give it live.

Drop the recording into Routinly and you get a numbered, screenshot-illustrated guide of everything you just showed. Five minutes of review later, it's in your onboarding library. You've turned a one-off performance into a permanent asset, for roughly zero extra effort.

## Build the library one task at a time

Don't schedule a documentation sprint — they don't survive contact with the calendar. Instead, apply one rule: **any walkthrough given live gets recorded.** Within a month or two of normal onboarding activity, the library builds itself:

- Accessing and using each core tool
- The deploy / release process
- Handling a support escalation
- Expense reports, time off, the admin basics
- The tribal-knowledge workarounds nobody remembers to mention

New hires work through guides at their own pace and only book time with teammates for the genuinely interactive parts — questions, context, judgment calls. That's the "twice as fast": less waiting for someone's calendar, less re-asking, more self-serve.

## Keep it current without a maintenance rota

Stale onboarding docs are worse than none — they teach the old process with confidence. Video-first documentation helps here too: when a tool changes, re-record the two-minute task and regenerate the guide. Updating a doc costs the same as creating one, which is to say: almost nothing.

The teams that do this stop dreading onboarding season. The walkthrough happens once — on video — and every hire after that gets the same source of truth.
